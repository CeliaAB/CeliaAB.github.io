My original culture values community and what an individual can contribute, rather than the individual. My adopted culture focuses on the individual, so I am conflicted most of the time. I value the individual and what he/she represents, but not at the expense of the community around them.

In my original culture, success is defined by financial and social standing. In NZ people care about and pursue goals such as work-life balance and self-fulfilment. It has moved me towards a way of approaching things, as opposed to focussing on outcomes - on "the journey" rather than "the destination".

Example from my Experience where I was trying to work productively with others where there was resistance or tension, and the Strategies I used

My career mainly consisted of leading large scale system change projects. Unless their business is doing projects, companies are not in the business of change, they are in the business of doing business. Every project is a disruption to that business, but kicked off by some need to change, which could be technological, environmental, commercial or any number of reasons. That is, an imperative for change. However, they are still in the business of doing business. As such, every project experiences resistance or tension.

Also, as immediate managers are evaluated on the performance of their part of the business, it is difficult for them to release good operational staff to projects for two reasons. The first is that businesses rarely have people to spare, so releasing experienced staff to a project means a gap needs to be filled by less experienced personnel. The second is that projects are usually run by non-operational people and are therefore not within the manager's control.

In this environment, large scale change happens.

The key to effective change is effective communication – probably the single biggest difference between success and failure. People are generally reasonable, very few will resist change when faced with the imperatives that drove that change, and the result that the change will bring.

Communication includes the definition of the change itself – not only its outcome, but how the change will come about. This is “the plan”.

The strategy used is therefore define, communicate, communicate, communicate again and get agreement. If you don’t define it, you can’t explain it; if you can’t explain it, you can’t communicate it; if you can’t communicate it, you can’t get agreement on it. It is important to state that agreement is not consensus. It is an agreed outcome from the stakeholders so people can move forward. Quite often, the word “buy-in” is used. I found that that follows agreement. You will never get buy-in when you don’t have agreement.

I would still use that strategy for large scale change. I really don’t see any alternatives for that magnitude of interruption.

In the Agile world, there is opportunity to build a product and show the product owner a finished alternative. Kind of make the change happen, then see if it is accepted. A viable alternative for product development, as opposed to enterprise wide change.

Though communication is after the work is done, it is still needed.
